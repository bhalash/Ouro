Bioprinting

The field of 3D printing is today one of the most dynamic areas of manufacturing technologies. Hobbyists and professional workshops alike can conceptualize a three-dimensional object, create a diagram of it with CAD software and bring it into reality then and there from their desk.

3D bioprinting is the evolutionary next step in this line of technology: spatially-controlled cell patterns printed layer by layer in arbitrary arrangements. Bioprinting can print both soft and hard tissues; muscles, nerves, bones, skin and eventually entire complex organs might eventually be printed from a laboratory bench.

The bioprinting process can follow one of several paths, given the differentiation between cells in different types of the body. Generally, the tissue is extruded in successive layers into a nutritious substrate through direct cell extrusion through a controlled syringe. The extruded material becomes the scaffold around which the targeted tissue grows.

Recent breakthroughs in this field include blood vessels, heart wall tissue and skin that might eventually be transplantable.

One of the key benefits of bioprinted tissue is that it can be grown from donor stem cells to thus reduce or even eliminate the chance of rejection or a lifelong dependence on immunosuppressive drugs.

Ouro: Making it Better

With the Ouro bioprinter we have developed a scalable technology platform to fabricate reproducible organ-specific biocompatible 3D biopolymer hydrogels (alginate, collagen, chitosan, fibrin). The Ouro Bioprinter will enable you to create stem cell niches for stem cell biology and has broad implications for designing better drug screening models, using fewer animals and developing approaches for personal medicines beyond genomic.
